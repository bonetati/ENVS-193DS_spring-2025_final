3a. The response variable is Swift Parrot nest box occupancy, where 1 indicates that a nest box was occupied by a Swift Parrot and 0 indicates that it was not occupied.

3b. Swift Parrots are a critically endangered species, while Common Starlings and Tree Martins are not. The study compares these species to determine whether artificial nest boxes are successful in supporting the conservation of Swift Parrots or unintentionally favoring the non-endagered species.

3c. The two seasons refer to data collected in 2016 and 2019. In 2016, Swift Parrots were known to be breeding in the area of study, while in 2019, they were not. This difference changes the availability and competition for nest boxes.

mod3 <- glm(sp ~ season, data = occdist, family = binomial) #Season only
mod4 <- glm(sp ~ edge_distance, data = occdist, family = binomial) #Distance only

This figure shows model-predicted probabilities of Swift Parrot nest box occupancy as a function of distance from the forest edge for the 2016 and 2019 breeding seasons. Lines represent predictions from a logistic regression including both season and distance as predictors. The shaded ribbons indicate 95% confidence intervals. 3k. Based on the model predictions, Swift Parrot nest box occupancy was highest near the forest edge and decreased when further away. In 2016, the predicted probability of occupancy was 0.48 at 0 meters and dropped to 0.12 at 900 meters. In 2019, occupancy was lower overall, with a probability of 0.30 at the forest edge and just 0.06 farther away. This shows a consistent negative relationship between distance from forest edge and nest box use. Biologically, this trend reflects the Swift Parrots patterns for nesting near forest edges. This is because it is where foraging opportunities and protective cover are more abundant.
